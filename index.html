The lab has shown that alcohol switches on mTORC1 in the nucleus accumbens, and that this drives the synaptic changes behind compulsive drinking. A recent result added a twist. Alongside turning up synaptic proteins, alcohol-driven mTORC1 also turns one thing down. Through the microRNA miR-34a-5p, it represses the translation of Aldolase A, an enzyme in the middle of glycolysis. mTORC1 is normally a switch that raises translation, so a repression arm is a real surprise.

That raised a simple question I could check from sequence alone. Is Aldolase A a one-off, or could the same microRNA reach the rest of glycolysis? If the whole pathway is being dialed down together, its enzymes should carry the same miR-34a-5p site in their 3'UTRs. If Aldolase A is special, they should not.

How the check works

A microRNA silences a gene by pairing its seed, positions 2 to 8, with a short matching site in the target's 3'UTR. For miR-34a-5p that site reads CACTGCC, with slightly stronger and weaker versions depending on the flanking base. I downloaded the 3'UTR sequence for each glycolytic enzyme from Ensembl, took the longest transcript per gene so the choice was not biased by the result, and counted these sites. Aldolase A, the enzyme the lab validated, serves as the positive control.

Analysis 2 — Is the signal specific, and does it spare the synaptic targets?

Method
Two controls: scrambled seeds, and the synaptic proteins mTORC1 turns up

A short motif can appear by chance, so I ran the same scan with scrambled seeds of the same length. I also scanned the synaptic proteins the lab reports mTORC1 translationally raises during alcohol use, since those should be escaping repression rather than falling under it.

Strong sites per gene · glycolysis vs synaptic mTORC1-up targets

The real seed lands about three times more often than scrambled seeds of the same composition, so the pattern is not just motif frequency. And the split runs in the expected direction. The glycolytic steps carry sites, while the synaptic drivers the lab highlights, GluA1 (Gria1), CaMKIIα, PSD-95 (Dlg4), and Homer1, carry none. The same alcohol signal that raises the synaptic machinery would then be free to lower the metabolic machinery through this microRNA.

Why this matters for the lab

The lab found one metabolic enzyme repressed by an alcohol-driven microRNA. This analysis suggests that enzyme is part of a larger pattern, and it names the specific glycolytic genes most likely to behave the same way. Each is a direct experiment: check the 3'UTR site by luciferase reporter, or test whether the enzyme's protein falls in the accumbens of alcohol-drinking mice while its mRNA stays flat. The work also fits the lab's broader picture, where alcohol raises the synaptic machinery of compulsive drinking while quietly lowering the cell's glycolytic capacity.

What I would bring

My background is in computational neurobiology and sequence analysis: pulling and parsing sequence data, writing motif and target scans, and building small tools that turn a one-off result into a screen across a whole pathway. I would be glad to extend this to the lab's own RNA-seq and translatome data, and to help prioritize which predicted targets are worth testing at the bench first.

Methods note

3'UTR sequences were downloaded from Ensembl BioMart (mouse), using the longest annotated 3'UTR per gene so the transcript choice is independent of the result. Sites are canonical miR-34a-5p seed matches: 8-mer (CACTGCCA), 7-mer-m8 (CACTGCC), and 7-mer-A1 (ACTGCCA). Strong site counts sum these three types. Scrambled controls use seeds of the same length and base composition. Across all sixteen glycolytic genes including isozymes, fourteen carry a site; two, the muscle PFK (Pfkm) and neuron-specific enolase (Eno2), do not, which is itself a lead about which enzymes are regulated this way. Seed matches are necessary but not sufficient for real targeting, so these are prioritized candidates for experimental follow-up, not confirmed targets. All scanning in the live tool runs in the browser on the embedded sequences.
